# Advisory 1.1 — Meeting Observation & Domain Expertise

**Advisory Item:** Observe Real Meetings Before Finalizing the Live Meeting Manager UX
**Resolution Method:** Firsthand domain expertise — 15 years as elected official, Town of Newcastle, ME
**Current Roles:** Select Board member; Chair, Planning Board
**Date:** March 2026

---

## Source Credibility

Rather than secondhand observation at 2–3 meetings, this input comes from approximately 15 years of direct participation as an elected official in Newcastle — serving on and currently serving on the Select Board, and currently serving as Chair of the Planning Board. This includes extensive experience with small town meetings both in Newcastle and other towns.

---

## Observation Questions — Answered from Experience

### 1. How far in advance do they know what's going to happen vs. what comes up unexpectedly?

Meetings follow the agenda items. The chair reads or introduces each item in order. The agenda is prepared in advance and sets the structure of the meeting.

**UX implication:** The live meeting manager's agenda-driven flow is correct. The primary path is linear — work through agenda items in order. The system should assume the agenda is known beforehand and that the meeting proceeds sequentially through it.

---

### 2. How do they handle motions that happen mid-conversation without a clear "now we're making a motion" signal?

More often than not, discussion begins about an item **before** a motion is made. Discussion usually continues until there is general consensus. Then one of two things happens:

- **Organic motion:** A member spontaneously makes a motion, it is seconded, and the chair calls for a vote and announces the result.
- **Chair-prompted motion:** The chair calls for a motion, another member makes it, it is seconded, and then proceeds as above.

There is no clean "now we are making a motion" moment. The transition from discussion to motion is fluid and often informal.

**UX implication:** This is the single most important design insight for the live meeting manager. The system **cannot** require a rigid sequence of [open item → discuss → motion → second → vote]. It must allow the operator to:
1. Begin recording discussion on an agenda item without a motion existing yet
2. Retroactively capture a motion that emerged organically from discussion
3. Record the motion text after the fact (the motion may have already been stated verbally before anyone writes it down)
4. Support the chair calling for a motion as a distinct interaction from a member volunteering one

The motion-capture UI should be interruptible and non-blocking — a "capture motion" action that can be triggered at any point during an active agenda item, not a required step in a sequence.

---

### 3. What does executive session entry actually look like in practice?

Executive session is a planned agenda item. The motion to enter executive session is usually **pre-written on the printed agenda** so the board gets the statutory language correct. One of the members reads and makes the motion as written. At that point:

1. The public is asked to leave the room
2. Video recording is stopped
3. The board conducts the executive session

**UX implication:** Executive session entry should be:
- Pre-configurable on the agenda item (with the motion text drafted during agenda building, not improvised live)
- A single-action trigger in the live meeting manager that: marks the transition timestamp, flags the recording status change, and records the pre-drafted motion
- The system should store the motion text from the agenda and allow confirmation rather than requiring re-entry during the meeting

---

### 4. How do they deal with members arriving late or leaving early mid-meeting?

This almost never happens.

**UX implication:** Late arrival / early departure tracking should exist (for quorum accuracy), but it should **not** be a prominent UI element. It can be an edge-case action — an "adjust attendance" option accessible but not in the primary flow. Do not design the attendance UI around the assumption that membership changes during a meeting.

---

### 5. What do they do when a vote is called before anyone writes down the motion text?

Two patterns exist:

**Pattern A — Real-time note-taker present:** If someone is taking notes in real time (a recording secretary), they may ask for a brief pause — "give me a minute" — to write down the motion word-for-word so they get it exactly right.

**Pattern B — Recorded meetings (increasingly common):** Some towns now record their meetings (video) so that the secretary, town clerk, or deputy clerk can review the footage afterward. They watch with frequent pauses and rewinding to transcribe the minutes accurately into a Word document or Google Doc.

**UX implication:** The live meeting manager must support both workflows:
- **Pattern A (live capture):** The motion text field should be easy to fill in during the meeting, with the understanding that the operator may need a moment. The system should not auto-advance or time-pressure the operator. A "motion pending — capture text" state should be comfortable to sit in.
- **Pattern B (post-meeting review):** The system should support a post-meeting editing mode where the operator can review what was captured live, fill in missing motion text, correct details, and finalize the structured record before generating minutes. This is not a secondary workflow — for many towns, this IS the primary workflow.

## Additional Observations

### Meeting Chair Role
- The chair is usually a Select Board member chosen from within the board each year
- The chair runs the meeting: reads/introduces agenda items, manages discussion, calls for motions and votes, announces results
- The chair is both a participant (voting member) and the meeting operator in most cases

**UX implication:** The system should assume the meeting operator (the person using the live meeting manager) may also be the chair or a board member. The UI should be operable with divided attention — the operator is simultaneously participating in discussion, not sitting aside as a dedicated note-taker.

### Minutes Production Workflow
- Real-time note-taking during the meeting is one approach, but reviewing recorded video afterward is increasingly common
- The review process involves frequent pauses and rewinding
- Output goes to Word or Google Doc format

**UX implication:** The live meeting manager should optimize for capturing the **structure** of what happened (which item, who moved, who seconded, vote result, timestamps) rather than requiring detailed prose during the meeting. The prose — the actual minutes text — can be generated or written afterward, either by the AI drafting system or by the clerk reviewing the recording. The live tool's job is to create an accurate structural skeleton.

## Proposed Feature: Distributed Audio Capture & AI-Driven Minutes

A significant capability opportunity emerged from the domain expertise review. The current spec envisions the live meeting manager as a structural capture tool operated by a single person. But if board members are already using laptops, phones, or tablets during meetings, their devices become potential recording nodes — and the meeting's audio record becomes something the system can own, not just something captured by an external camera.

### Concept

Each participant's device (board members, staff, town manager, legal counsel, and potentially members of the public during public comment) can optionally capture audio through the app. The system merges these individual audio streams into a composite meeting record — with speaker identification built in, because each stream is tied to a known participant's device.

This captured audio feeds directly into the AI minutes drafting pipeline, either in real time or post-meeting, to produce summary or detailed minutes automatically.

### Two Modes of Operation

#### Mode 1: Formal — "Push to Speak"

For boards that run more structured meetings or want tighter process control:

- **Request to speak:** Board members tap a "request to speak" control in the app, entering a speaker queue
- **Chair recognition:** The chair sees the queue in order received and recognizes speakers sequentially
- **Active speaker:** The recognized member's microphone is the primary capture source; they speak and then yield back to the chair through the app
- **Automatic flow capture:** The system logs the formal sequence — who requested, when recognized, when yielded — and this procedural record feeds directly into the minutes structure

**UX characteristics:**
- The chair's UI shows a speaker queue (ordered list of pending requests) with a "recognize" action
- The active speaker's UI shows a "yield" action
- Other members see their position in queue
- The system captures the parliamentary flow (recognized → spoke → yielded) as structured data, not just audio
- Well-suited for public hearings, contentious agenda items, or boards that follow Robert's Rules more closely

#### Mode 2: Informal — AI-Parsed Open Discussion

For the more common small-town meeting style where discussion flows naturally:

- **Passive recording:** All participating devices capture audio continuously (with consent) during the meeting
- **Speaker attribution:** Since each audio stream comes from a known device/participant, the system can attribute speech to individuals without needing voice fingerprinting — proximity and volume analysis identifies who is speaking on which device's mic
- **AI processing (real-time or post-meeting):**
  - *Real-time:* Transcription and speaker identification happen during the meeting; the operator sees a rolling transcript attributed to speakers, and can flag key moments (motions, votes) as they occur
  - *Post-meeting:* Audio streams are merged and processed after adjournment; the AI produces a draft transcript with speaker attribution, identifies motions/seconds/votes from the audio content, and generates minutes in the town's preferred style

**UX characteristics:**
- Minimal interaction required during the meeting — devices just need to be present and recording
- The operator's primary job remains structural capture (agenda item transitions, vote results), but the audio record fills in everything they miss
- Post-meeting review shifts from watching video and rewinding to reviewing an AI-generated transcript/draft and correcting it
- Well-suited for the typical informal small-town meeting flow described in the observations above

### How This Interacts with Existing Design Principles

| Principle | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Discussion-first, motion-second | Informal mode captures the discussion naturally; AI can retroactively identify where the motion emerged |
| Non-blocking motion capture | The operator can still flag motions manually, but the audio record provides a fallback if they miss it |
| Divided attention operator | This is the biggest win — if the chair is also the operator, distributed audio capture means they don't have to take notes at all during the meeting |
| Post-meeting editing is primary | AI-generated minutes from audio become the starting point instead of a structural skeleton |
| Structure over prose | The live tool still captures structure; but now audio provides the prose layer automatically |

### Technical Considerations (to be explored further)

- **Audio capture API:** Web Audio API (browser) and native audio APIs (React Native) for device-level recording
- **Storage:** Audio files are potentially large; need a strategy for temporary local storage during meeting, then upload to Supabase Storage post-meeting or streamed during
- **Transcription service:** Whisper (self-hosted or API), Deepgram, or AssemblyAI for speech-to-text; speaker diarization is needed for the informal mode
- **Privacy & consent:** Maine is a one-party consent state for recording, but municipal meetings are public proceedings — recording is generally expected. The app should still have clear "recording active" indicators and consent flows
- **Executive session boundary:** Audio recording **must** stop automatically when executive session is triggered — this is legally required. The push-to-speak mode and informal recording both need hard stops tied to the executive session transition
- **Bandwidth:** Real-time streaming of multiple audio tracks requires reasonable connectivity; offline-first recording with post-meeting upload is the safer default for town meeting rooms with unreliable WiFi
- **Speaker attribution accuracy:** In informal mode, if members are sitting close together, a single device's mic may pick up multiple voices. The system needs to handle this gracefully — possibly prompting the operator to confirm attribution in ambiguous cases during post-meeting review

### Phasing Recommendation

This is a major capability that should **not** be part of the Phase 1 MVP. Recommended phasing:

- **Phase 1:** Live meeting manager with manual structural capture only (the current design). Get this right first.
- **Phase 2:** Add optional single-device audio recording (the operator's device records the whole meeting, similar to current video recording but audio-only through the app). AI transcription generates a draft from this single source. This is the minimum viable version of the concept.
- **Phase 3:** Distributed multi-device recording with speaker attribution. Push-to-speak formal mode. Full AI-driven minutes pipeline from merged audio streams.

This phasing lets the product deliver value immediately (Phase 1), prove the AI minutes pipeline with the simplest recording setup (Phase 2), and then expand to the full distributed vision once the foundation is solid (Phase 3).

**Item 1.1 is resolved.** The domain expertise provided exceeds the depth that external observation at 2–3 meetings would have yielded. The specific UX implications above should be incorporated into the live meeting manager wireframes and component design before development begins.

### Key Design Principles Derived

1. **Discussion-first, motion-second.** The UI must not enforce a motion-before-discussion sequence.
2. **Non-blocking motion capture.** Motion text entry should be interruptible and retroactive.
3. **Executive session is pre-planned.** Motion text should flow from agenda building, not be improvised.
4. **Attendance is stable.** Late arrivals and early departures are rare edge cases, not primary flows.
5. **Post-meeting editing is a primary workflow.** Many towns will capture structure live and refine afterward.
6. **Divided attention operator.** The person running the tool is often also chairing or participating in the meeting.
7. **Structure over prose.** The live tool captures what happened; prose comes later.
8. **Devices are recording nodes.** Every participant's device is a potential audio capture source — the system should be architected from day one to accommodate distributed recording, even if the feature ships later.
9. **Two formality modes.** Formal push-to-speak and informal open discussion are both valid meeting styles; the system should support both rather than forcing one model.
10. **Audio is the minutes source.** The long-term vision replaces "review video and rewind" with "review AI-generated transcript and correct" — a fundamentally better workflow for clerks.
